Project 2: Redesigning Nightingale's Coxcomb: A Humanistic Exploration of Health Expenditure and Life Expectancy

This project examines the relationship between government health expenditure (% of GDP) and life expectancy at birth (years) using data from the World Bank (2000-2022). Following a humanistic approach , the goal is to treat visualization as an interpretive dialogue rather than a neutral display.

Project 3: Financial Storytelling: Integrating Data and Narrative in Policy Analysis

This report analyzes the Financial Times' interactive "Trump Tariffs" tracker as a case study in effective data storytelling. The analysis focuses on how the project uses a rebased S&P 500 chart to concretely visualize the impact of political policies on financial markets. It highlights the importance of integrating quantitative data with qualitative narratives and news feeds to make complex economic issues relatable and demonstrate their real-world consequences across industries and countries.

Project 4: The Real Cost of Inflation in Canada: How Rising Prices Affect Canadians in 2025

This analysis addresses the "real cost" of inflation in Canada, tracing the CPI trend from its 8% peak in 2022 to the projected rates in 2025. The core of the project visualizes how rising prices differentially impact various age groups, highlighting high levels of financial stress and housing affordability concerns, particularly among younger Canadians. The report combines data visualization with practical steps for managing economic pressure.

Project 5: AI Automation & The Future of Work in the GTA: A Data Story on Youth Employment

This collaborative project is a data narrative examining the impact of AI automation on youth employment and financial stability in the Greater Toronto Area (GTA). It visualizes data on rising youth unemployment and the automation risk across various job skill levels. The project uses two case studies (Kevin and Alice) to illustrate how AI is changing hiring expectations and deepening the gap between minimum wage and the cost of living, even for university graduates.
